Abnormal Uterine Bleeding/Adolescents/Cancer Treatment

General

  • Risk thromboembolism (eg combined oral contraceptive contraindicated with bone marrow or stem cell transplant).1
  • Need for contraception.1
  • Emetogenic potential.1

Acute Management

  • Medroxyprogesterone acetate 60-80 mg orally BID until bleeding controlled and then progestin taper.1
  • Norethindrone acetate (Aygestin) 10-20 mg orally every 8 hours until bleeding controlled and then progestin taper.1

Progestin taper: Medroxyprogesterone acetate 10 mg orally every 4-6 hours as needed to manage bleeding.1

  • Continuous combined oral contraceptive taper. Monophasic 30-50 mcg ethinyl estradiol/norgestrel or levonorgestrel every 6-8 hours and taper as needed to manage bleeding. Skip placebos and take continuously.1
  • Premarin 25 mg IV every 4 hours to decrease bleeding then continuous combined oral contraceptive taper as above. Consider antiemetics.1
  • Leuprolide acetate with norethindrone acetate 5 mg daily add-back as below.1
  • Progestin-only therapy as below.1
  • Continuous combined oral contraceptive as below.1
  • Alternate acute management (Progestin | Estrogen).1
  • Possible pelvic ultrasound for endometrial thickness to guide treatment.1
  • Possible antifibrinolytics (oral or IV tranexamic acid).1
  • Possible intrauterine foley.1
  • Possible suction curettage with Levonorgestrel-releasing IUD.1

Menstrual Suppression

  • Use prophylactically or after acute management.1
  • Advantages:1
    • Best used for menstrual suppression. High rates of amenorrhea (up to 96%).1
    • Not prothrombotic.1
    • Possible chemotherapy protective effect on premature ovarian failure but no evidence preserves fertility.1
  • Disadvantages:1
    • Not contraception.1
    • Initial flare with AUB 1st 2-3 weeks. Norethindrone acetate 5 mg daily may decrease. If severe can use acute management options.1
    • Low estrogen effects of vasomotor symptoms and bone loss. Decreased with concurrent use of add-back therapy.1
  • Regimens:1
    • 3.75 mg IM monthly / 11.5 mg IM every 12 weeks / Subcutaneous form available if IM hematoma concerns.1
    • Add-back: Norethindrone acetate 5 mg daily reasonable to start with onset of therapy.1
    • Add-back: Norethindrone acetate 5 mg + conjugated equine estrogen 0.625 mg daily has more benefits on bone density if not contraindicated.1
  • Oral therapy and amenorrhea rate like leuprolide acetate but no current guidance for use with cancer treatment.1
  • Medroxyprogesterone acetate:1
    • 10–20 mg orally/day.1
    • Not contraception.1
  • Norethindrone acetate (Aygestin):1
    • 5-15 mg orally/day.1
    • Not contraception.1
  • Drospirenone (Slynd):1
    • 4 mg orally/day.1
    • Contraception but more unscheduled bleeding.1
  • Norethindrone (Micronor, etc.):1
    • 0.35 mg orally/day.1
    • Contraception but more unscheduled bleeding.1
  • Depot Medroxyprogesterone Acetate IM:1
    • 50-70% amenorrheic in 12-24 months but initially high break through bleeding rate.1
    • Subcutaneous form available if IM hematoma concerns.1
  • LARC:1
    • Levonorgestrel-releasing IUD:1
      • 32% amenorrheic 3 months.1
      • Can be used in immunosuppressed.1
      • If pre-existing and bleeding can use progestin taper.1
    • Etonogestrel single-rod implant:1
      • Irregular bleeding limits use for menstrual suppression.1
      • If pre-existing and bleeding can use progestin taper.1

Progestin taper: Medroxyprogesterone acetate 10 mg orally every 4-6 hours as needed to manage bleeding.1

  • Emetogenic.1
  • Risk VTE (eg Bone marrow and stem cell transplants have increased risk hyperbilirubinemia and hepatic veno-occlusive disease).1
  1. Options for prevention and management of menstrual bleeding in adolescent patients undergoing cancer treatment. ACOG Committee Opinion No. 817. American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists. Obstet Gynecol 2021;137:e7–15.
