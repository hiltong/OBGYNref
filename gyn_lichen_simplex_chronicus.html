  • References
  • Nonscarring chronic pruritic inflammatory disease of the vulva.1
  • Primary condition.1
  • Secondary complication from other pruritic condition:1
    • Environmental factors such as heat, sweating, irritation from clothing or products.1
    • Dermatologic conditions such as candidiasis, contact dermatitis, lichen sclerosus.1
  • Chronic pruritis and scratching.1
  • Usually in evening/night interfering with sleep.1
  • Usually occurs mid adult life and later.1
  • 75% have or close family have a history of seasonal allergies, asthma, or childhood eczema.1
  • Erythematous, scaling, or lichenified plaques. 1
  • If long duration thick, leathery, “bark-like” skin.1
  • Excoriations often present.1
  • Avoid vulvar irritants and allergens.1
  • Use mild soaps, but avoid use on the vulva.1
  • Cleanse the vulva with water only.1
  • Gently pat the vulva dry after bathing.1
  • Apply preservative-free, unscented or fragrance-free emollient to hold moisture in the skin and improve barrier function.1
  • Use Peri-Care bottle to rinse after urination.1
  • Use 100% cotton, unscented or fragrance-free menstrual pads.1
  • Use adequate lubrication for intercourse (unscented or fragrance-free silicone-based lubricants recommended).
  • Daytime:
    • Nonsedating antihistamines (cetirizine (Zyrtec), Loratadine (Claritin)).1
    • SSRIs (fluoxetine, paroxetine, sertraline, or citalopram).1
  • Nighttime:
    • Sedating antihistamine (Hydroxyzine).1
    • TCAs (Doxepin, Amitriptyline (Elavil)).xxx

    • Desonide 0.05%1
    • Hydrocortisone 1%, 2.5%1

Use high or medium potency.1

Ointment preferred.1

Avoid topical antipruritic medications as can cause allergic contact dermatitis.1
