Hydatidiform Mole

General

  • 1-2/1000 pregnancies in North America, Europe, and other developed nations.1
  • 2–3 x higher Asia, Brazil, and India.1
  • Reproductive age extremes:
    • < 15 years of age.1
    • > 45 years of age (increased 5–10 x).1
  • Previous molar pregnancy:
    • One previous 1-2% risk of second.1
    • Two previous 15-20% risk of third.1
    • Previous complete mole higher risk of recurrence.1
    • When reoccurs usually histology is the same1
  • Complete moles:
    • Diploid karyotypes (46 XX or46 XY) that are entirely paternal from fertilization of an ovum without maternal DNA by:1
      • 80% duplication of the haploid genome from a single sperm.1
      • 20% dispermic fertilization.1
    • hCG levels often above 100,000 IU/L secondary to excessive trophoblastic proliferation.1
    • Ultrasound:
      • Hydropic villi.1
      • Theca lutein cysts.1
  • Partial mole:
    • Triploid karyotypes:1
      • Dispermic fertilization of an ovum with maternal DNA.1
    • hCG levels rarely above 100,000 IU/L secondary to less trophoblastic proliferation.1
    • Ultrasound:
      • Focal hydropic villi.1
      • Nonviable fetus.1
      • Increased transverse to anteroposterior ratio of gestational sac.1
  • Often 1st trimester secondary to hCG and ultrasound so rare to see excessive uterine size, anemia, preeclampsia, hyperemesis, hyperthyroidism, and respiratory failure.1
  • Often require additional pathology tests such as immunohistochemistry for the maternally expressed P57 gene to differentiate complete (P57 absent) vs partial moles/hydropic miscarriages (P57 present).1
  • Even with earlier diagnosis there has been no change in incidence of post-molar gestational trophoblastic neoplasm.1
  • Complete mole 20%.1
  • Partial mole 4%.1
  • Increased risk for post-molar gestational trophoblastic neoplasm:
    • hCG > 100,000 IU/L. 1
    • Large uterine size and theca lutein cysts.1
    • > 40 years of age:1
      • 85% risk if 40–49 years of age and hCG levels > 175,000 mIU/mL 1

Management

  • Suction dilation and curettage:1
    • Ultrasound guidance may increase completeness of evacuation.1
    • Uterotonic agents as needed.1
    • Rhogam if Rh negative.1
    • Complications rare at early gestational age but be on alert for hemorrhage and pulmonary compromise.1
  • Hysterectomy:
    • Decreases post-molar gestational trophoblastic neoplasm by up to 80% over suction curettage:1
      • Eliminates risk of locally invasive disease.1
      • Still risk of occult metastases so need hCG surveillance.1
    • Candidates:
      • Completed childbearing.1
      • Concerns about compliance with hCG follow up.1
      • Increased risk for post-molar gestational trophoblastic neoplasm (see above).1
  • Medically induced evacuations not indicated.1
  • Prophylactic chemotherapy not recommended.1

Post-evacuation surveillance

  • Reliable contraception:1
  • Weekly hCG until negative (or diagnosis of gestational trophoblastic neoplasm) then:1
    • Complete mole monthly for 3 months.1
    • Partial mole monthly for 1 month.1
    • ACOG recommends monthy for 6 months for complete and partial mole.1
