Genital Herpes

General

  • ~21% women HSV-2 seropositive (would be higher if included genital HSV-1).1
  • Only 5–15% patients recognize being infected.1
  • Primary first-episode infection:
    • HSV-1 or HSV-2 in lesions and no antibodies to either viral type in serum.1
  • Nonprimary first-episode:
    • One viral type in lesions and antibodies to the other viral type in serum1
  • Recurrent infection (reactivation of viral replication):
    • HSV-1 or HSV-2 in lesions and antibodies to the same viral type in serum.1
      • Recurrent ulcerations.1
      • Subclinical asymptomatic shedding.1
  • Double-stranded DNA virus:1
    • HSV-1 (Glycoprotein G1 in lipid bilayer envelope):1
      • Causes orolabial HSV.1
      • Increasing common cause of genital HSV particularly in young women.1
    • HSV-2 (Glycoprotein G2 in lipid bilayer envelope):1
      • Uncommon to cause orolabial HSV.i
      • Usually just causes genital infection.1
      • Causes most genital HSV.1
  • Incubation 2-12 days after acquiring.1
  • Type-specific antibodies develop in 2–3 weeks and persist indefinitely.1
  • Viral detection methods:1
    • Viral culture:
      • Low sensitivity:
        • 80% positive primary lesions.1
        • 40% positive with recurrent lesions.1
    • Polymerase chain reaction detection HSV antigens:
      • 3-5 more sensitive than culture.1
  • Antibody detection methods:1
    • HSV-2 antibodies:
      • Genital HSV-2 infection 1
    • HSV-1 antibodies:
      • Orolabial HSV-1 infection or genital HSV-1 infection.1

Pregnancy

  • Consider offering c-section if primary or nonprimary first-episode in 3rd trimester secondary to possibility of prolonged viral shedding.1
  • Consider continuing treatment until delivery if primary or nonprimary first-episode in 3rd trimester.1
  • ~10% of HSV-2 seronegative wome are at risk secondary to HSV-2 seropositive partners.1
  • ~2% seronegative women acquire HSV-1 or HSV-2 during pregnancy.1
  • Most new infections are asymptomatic.1
  • 1/3 new infections occur in each trimester.1
  • 15% first-episode infections in pregnancy are recurrent infections.1
  • ~75% have at least one recurrence in pregnancy.1
  • ~14% have prodromal symptoms or recurrence at delivery.1
  • ~1/3 -1/2 secondary to HSV-1.1
  • Classified:1
    • 25% disseminated disease (mortality 30%).1
    • 30% CNS disease (mortality 4%).1
    • 45% skin, eyes, mouth.1
  • ~20% survivors long-term neurologic problems.1
  • Primary or nonprimary first-episode:1
    • Acyclovir:1
      • 400 mg po TID x 7-10 days.1
      • Can extend longer than 10 days if lesions not healed.1
      • Option to continue to delivery if occurs in 3rd trimester.1
    • Valacyclovir:1
      • 1 g po BID x 7-10 days.1
      • Can extend longer than 10 days if lesions not healed.1
      • Option to continue to delivery if occurs in 3rd trimester.1
  • Symptomatic recurrent episode:1
    • Acyclovir:1
      • 400 mg po TID x 5 days or 800 mg po BID x 5d days.1
    • Valacyclovir:1
      • 500 mg po BID x 3 days or 1 g po daily x 5 days.1
  • Suppresion:1
    • Acyclovir:1
      • 400 mg po TID starting at 36 weeks ubtil delivery.1
    • Valacyclovir:1
      • 500 mg po BID starting at 36 weeks ubtil delivery.1
  • Severe disease:1
    • Acyclovir1
      • 5-10 mg/kg IV every 8 hrs x 2-7 days and then po primary regimen to complete 10 days.1
