Genital Herpes

General

  • Not really known because not reportable disease but one of most common STIs.1
  • ~21% women HSV-2 seropositive (would be higher if included genital HSV-1).1
  • Only 5–15% recognize infection.1
  • Double-stranded DNA virus with 2 subtypes:1
    • HSV-1 (Glycoprotein G1 in lipid bilayer envelope):1
      • Causes orolabial HSV.1
      • Increasing common cause of genital HSV particularly in young women.1
    • HSV-2 (Glycoprotein G2 in lipid bilayer envelope):1
      • Uncommon to cause orolabial HSV.i
      • Usually just causes genital infection.1
      • Causes most genital HSV.1
  • Transmitted by direct contact with mucous membranes or abraided skin.1
  • Primary first-episode infection:
    • HSV-1 or HSV-2 in lesions and no antibodies to either viral type in serum.1
  • Nonprimary first-episode:
    • One viral type in lesions and antibodies to the other viral type in serum1
  • Recurrent infection (reactivation of viral replication):
    • HSV-1 or HSV-2 in lesions and antibodies to the same viral type in serum.1
      • Recurrent ulcerations.1
      • Subclinical asymptomatic shedding.1
  • Incubation 2-12 days after acquiring.1
  • Type-specific antibodies develop in 2–3 weeks and persist indefinitely.1
  • Viral detection methods:1
    • Viral culture:
      • Low sensitivity:
        • 80% positive primary lesions.1
        • 40% positive with recurrent lesions.1
    • Polymerase chain reaction detection of HSV antigens:
      • 3-5 more sensitive than culture.1
  • Antibody detection methods:1
    • HSV-2 antibodies:
      • Genital HSV-2 infection 1
    • HSV-1 antibodies:
      • Orolabial HSV-1 infection or genital HSV-1 infection.1
  • Positive viral detection and negative serology.1
  • Seroconversion.1
  • Acyclovir:
    • Nucleoside analogue that enters infected cells and inhibits viral thymidine kinase to inhibit DNA replication. 1
    • Safe in all trimesters of pregnancy. 1
    • Rare to develop resistance in immunocompetent patients (0.3-.06%) but more common in immunocompromised (6–7%).1
  • Valacyclovir:
    • Prodrug of acyclovir which is converted to acyclovir in the liver.1
    • Should have same safety profile as acyclovir.1
    • More bioavailable so can take less often than acyclovir.1
    • More expensive than acyclovir.1
  • Famciclovir:
    • No data on the use in pregnancy.1

Pregnancy

  • Consider offering c-section if primary or nonprimary first-episode in 3rd trimester secondary to possibility of prolonged cervical viral shedding.1
  • Consider continuing treatment until delivery if primary or nonprimary first-episode in 3rd trimester.1
  • Women at risk:
    • ~10% of HSV-2 seronegative women have HSV-2 seropositive partners.1
    • Most new infections are asymptomatic.1
  • Incidence:
    • ~2% seronegative women acquire HSV-1 or HSV-2 during pregnancy.1
    • 15% first-episode infections in pregnancy are recurrent infections.1
  • Timing new infections:
    • 1/3 new infections occur in each trimester.1
  • Risk if occurs at delivery:
    • 40-80% risk vertical transmission with vaginal delivery.1
      • Less transplacental passage herpes antibodies. 1
      • Longer and higher levels of viral shedding than with recurrent disease.1
      • Cervical shedding occurs in 90%.1
  • ~75% at least one recurrence in pregnancy.1
  • ~14% have prodromal symptoms or recurrence at delivery.1
    • Risk if occurs at delivery:
    • 3% risk vertical transmission with vaginal delivery.2
  • Primary outbreak at delivery:
    • 40-80%.1
  • Recurrent outbreak:
    • 3%.2
    • Asymptomatic with no active lesions:
    • 2/10,000.2
  • ~1/3 -1/2 secondary to HSV-1.1
  • Classified:1
    • 25% disseminated disease (mortality 30%).1
    • 30% CNS disease (mortality 4%).1
    • 45% skin, eyes, mouth.1
  • ~20% survivors long-term neurologic problems.1
  • Primary or nonprimary first-episode:1
    • Acyclovir:1
      • 400 mg po TID x 7-10 days.1
      • Can extend longer than 10 days if lesions not healed.1
      • Option to continue to delivery if occurs in 3rd trimester.1
    • Valacyclovir:1
      • 1 g po BID x 7-10 days.1
      • Can extend longer than 10 days if lesions not healed.1
      • Option to continue to delivery if occurs in 3rd trimester.1
  • Symptomatic recurrent episode:1
    • Acyclovir:1
      • 400 mg po TID x 5 days or 800 mg po BID x 5d days.1
    • Valacyclovir:1
      • 500 mg po BID x 3 days or 1 g po daily x 5 days.1
  • Suppresion:1
    • Acyclovir:1
      • 400 mg po TID starting at 36 weeks ubtil delivery.1
    • Valacyclovir:1
      • 500 mg po BID starting at 36 weeks ubtil delivery.1
  • Severe genital disease or disseminated infections:1
    • Acyclovir1
      • 5-10 mg/kg IV every 8 hrs x 2-7 days and then po primary regimen to complete 10 days.1
  • If history of genital herpes start at 36 weeks.1
    • 75% decrease recurrence at delivery.2
    • 40% decrease in C-section for recurrence.2
  • If primary outbreaks in the third trimester continue until delivery.1
  • C-section indicated:
    • Active genital lesions or prodromal symptoms.1
    • Can offer if primary or nonprimary first-episode genital HSV infection in 3rd trimester secondary to possibility of prolonged viral shedding .1
  • C-section not indicated:
    • Nongenital lesions back, thigh, buttock (can cover with occlusive dressing).1
  • If expectant management antiviral therapy recommended.1

  2. PROLOG: Obstetrics, Eighth Edition. Question 83.
