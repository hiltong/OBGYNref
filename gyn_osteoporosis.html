Osteoporosis

Background

  • In U.S. women over 50 one out of two will develop osteoporotic fracture.1
  • Calcium RDA:
    • 1,000 mg/day 19-50 years of age.1
    • 1,200 mg/day >: 50 years of age.1
  • Vitamin D RDA:
    • 600 international units/day to age 70 years of age1
    • 800 international units/day >: 70 years of age.1
  • T-score:
    • Number of STD units above or below the mean average BMD in a health young-adult (non-age adjusted) caucasian (non-race adjusted) female reference population.1
    • Used to diagnose osteoporosis in the postmenopausal women.1
      • Normal: ≥ -1.0.1
      • Low bone mass (osteopenia): Between -1.0 and -2.5.1
      • Osteoporosis: ≤ -2.5.1
  • Z-score:
    • Number of STD units above or below the mean BMD of a reference population of the same sex, age, and ethnicity.1
    • Used to identify premenopausal women at risk of secondary osteoporosis (eg, medications or conditions).1
      • Abnormal: Z-score ≤ −2.0.1

Screening

  • Intrinsic:
    • Age (≥ 65 indication for BMD).1
    • BMI < 20 or weight <: 127 lb.1
    • Previous fragility fracture diagnostic of osteoporosis even if absent or normal BMD.1
  • Habits:
    • Smoking.1
    • > 3 drinks a day.1
  • Family history:
    • Parent with hip or spine fracture.1
  • Conditions:
    • HIV, anorexia nervosa, diabetes mellitus (type 1 and type 2), gastric bypass, hyperparathyroidism, hypocalcemia, premature menopause , primary ovarian insufficiency, renal impairment, rheumatoid arthritis, Turner’s syndrome, vitamin D deficiency.1
  • Medications:
    • Antiepileptic drugs, antiretroviral drugs, aromatase inhibitors, chemotherapy, DMPA, glucocorticoids, gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonists and antagonists, heparin.1
  • Height loss may indicate:
    • Possible asymptomatic vertebral fracture.1
    • Increases risk of nonvertebral fracture1
  • The National Osteoporosis Foundation recommends vertebral imaging (can be done on DXA machine) if:
    • 1.5 inch (4 cm) loss from peak height at 20 years of age.1
    • 0.8 inch (2cm) loss from a previous measurement.1
  • Used in adults ≥ 40 years of age with or without a femoral neck BMD score to estimate the 10-year risk of hip fracture and the 10-year probability of a major osteoporotic fracture (clinical spine, forearm, hip, or shoulder fracture) .1
  • Used to:
    • Determine the need for BMD testing in postmenopausal patients < 65 years of age with at least one of the risk factors.1
    • Determine the need for treatment with a T-score between −1.0 and −2.5.1
  • FRAX Fracture Risk Assessment Tool
  • Initial screening 2 groups:
    • By age: Postmenopausal women ≥ 65 years of age.1
    • By risk assessment tool: Postmenopausal women < 65 years and at increased risk of osteoporosis by a formal clinical risk assessment tool (eg, FRAX).1
      • Order of evaluation:
        • 1st: FRAX if < 65 years old and at least one risk factor.1
        • 2nd: BMD if 10-year FRAX risk of major osteoporotic fracture > 8.4% (equal to the risk of a 65-year-old low-risk white woman).1
  • Repeat screening (usually no sooner than 2 years) if:
    • Initial BMD near threshold of treatment.1
    • Significant risk factor changes.1

Diagnosis

  • Previous fragility fracture (includes asymptomatic vertebral fracture) even if normal BMD.1
  • T-score ≤ −2.5.1
    • Femoral neck and lumbar spine most accurate.1
    • Distal 1/3 radius if can't use one or both of the above (eg, bilateral hip replacements and/or lumbar spine surgery).1
  • T-score between –1.0 and –2.5 and FRAX with 3% 10-year risk of hip fracture or 20% 10-year risk of major osteoporotic fracture (clinical spine, forearm, hip, or shoulder fracture).1
