Hepatitis C

General

  • Most common reported bloodborne infection in US.1
  • ~ 1% to 4% of pregnant women in U.S.1
  • Acute infection (1st 6 months of infection):3
    • Symptoms:
      • 75% asymptomatic.3
      • 25% symptoms (abdominal pain, nausea, anorexia, jaundice, and malaise).3
    • Serology:
      • Hepatitis C RNA (viremia) detected 1-3 weeks and indicates active infection.3
        • 15-45% clear within 6 months without treatment.3
      • Anti-hepatitis C antibodies develop in 2-6 months and positive for life 3
  • Chronic hepatitis (55-85%):3
    • Usually asymptomatic despite ongoing liver damage.3
    • 15-30% develop cirrhosis within 20 years.3
      • 27% with cirrhosis develop hepatocellular carcinoma within 10 years (only 5 % if successfully treated).3
      • Hepatocellular carcinoma is primary cause of death from hepatitis C infection with a median length of survival 20 months.3
  • ~5% perinatal transmission (most common cause hepatitis C infection in children).3
    • 1/3 - 1/2 occur prior to last month of pregnancy.3
    • 1/2 - 2/3 last month of pregnancy or at birth.3
  • Increase in fetal growth restriction and low birthweight.3
  • Hepatitis B:
    • Unvaccinated 6-30% depending on HBeAg status.2
    • Vaccinated virtually no risk.2
  • Hepatitis C:
    • ~1.8%.2
  • HIV:
    • ~0.3%.2

Screening

  • Becoming more common in women of childbearing age.1
  • Effective treatments available.1
  • All adults at least once in their lifetime (ideal to diagnose prepregnancy).1
  • First prenatal visit each pregnancy.1
    • Identifies mothers that need treatment.1
    • Identifies infants that need testing.1
  • With intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy if < 24 weeks or bile acids ≥ 100 mmol/L.3
    • 20x higher risk of intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy.3
  • Initial screen: Obtain anti-hepatitis C antibodies.3
  • If antibodies positive: Obtain hepatitis C RNA.3
    • Positive antibodies and positive RNA:
      • Active acute or chronic infection (if patient with positive RNA had a negative RNA in the previous 6 months can diagnoses acute infection otherwise canâ€™t differentiate acute from chronic infection).3
    • Positive antibodies and negative RNA:
      • Past infection cleared or false positive. Test with a different antibody assay to determine which.3

Antepartum

  • Positive antibodies and positive RNA:3
    • Active acute or chronic infection (if patient with positive RNA had a negative RNA in the previous 6 months can diagnoses acute infection otherwise canâ€™t differentiate acute from chronic infection).3
  • Positive antibodies and negative RNA:3
    • Past infection cleared or false positive antibody test (test with a different antibody assay to determne).3
  • Lab:
    • Quantitative hepatitis C RNA, hepatitis C genotype (6 different genotypes).3
    • Liver function tests (AST, ALT, bilirubin), albumin, platelet count, prothrombin time3
    • Screen for HIV, syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia, hepatitis B and immunity to hepatitis A.3
    • Transmission prevention.3
    • Avoid alcohol.3
  • Vaccinate if not immune for hepatitis A and B.3
  • Treatment:
    • Initiation of treatment not approved in pregnancy presently except in trials.3
    • If conceives while being treated shared decision making about continuing.3
  • Limited but reassuring data on prenatal diagnostic testing.3
  • Fetal growth assessment 3rd trimester secondary to association with fetal growth restriction and low birthweight.3
  • Antenatal fetal testing not indicated.3

Intrapartum

  • If possible avoid internal fetal monitors and early AROM.3
  • Avoid expectant management PROM at term.3
  • C-section not indicated.3

    • If positive hepatitis C antibody and negative hepatitis C antigen and not proven to be false positive by additional hepatitis C antibody test theoretical possibility of intermittent viral shedding and may be best to follow these precautions.3

Postpartum

  • Breastfeeding OK unless cracked or bleeding nipples.3
  • Treatment after finished breastfeeding.3
