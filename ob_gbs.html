Newborn Early Onset GBS Disease Prevention

General

  • Leading cause of newborn infection.1
  • Also called Streptococcus agalactiae.1
  • Facultative gram-positive.1
  • Vaginal and rectal colonization can be intermittent, transient or persistent.1
  • Early onset disease:1
    • 1st 7 days after birth (usually 1st 12-48 hours).1
    • Sepsis, pneumonia or less common meningitis1
    • Vertical transmission.1
    • 72% cases occur in term newborns1
    • Mortality 2.1% term/19.2% preterm.1
  • Late onset disease:1
    • 7 days - 3 months after birth.1
    • Bacteremia, meningitis or organ/soft tissue infection.1
    • Horizontal transmission from usually mother or other source.1
  • 10-30% pregnant women have vaginal or rectal colonization.1
    • 50% colonized women transmit GBS to newborn.1
      • 1-2% newborns with GBS develop early-onset GBS disease without intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis.1
      • Incidence newborn early onset GBS disease 0.23/1000 livebirths 2015 (80% drop from 1.8/1000 livebiths in 1990s).1
  1. Maternal vaginal-rectal colonization at time of labor most important.1
    • Positive GBS culture.1
    • GBS bacteriuria.1
    • Previuous newborn early onset GBS disease.1
  2. < 37 0/7 weeks.1
  3. Very low birth weight (< 1500 grams | 3 lb 5 oz)1
  4. Prolonged ROM.1
  5. Amnionitis.1
  6. Young maternal age.1
  7. Black race.1

Diagnosis

  • 36 0/7 - 37 6/7 weeks, changed because:1
    • If labor before 37 0/7 weeks and unknown GBS status antiobiotic prophylaxis recommended.1
    • Reflects GBS colonization very accurately if performed within 5 weeks of birth so covers up to ≥ 41 0/7 weeks. Accuracy decreases after 5 weeks ("5 week screening accuracy window").1
    • "likely to reduce the reported incidence of discrepant antepartum culture results and colonization status at the time of birth".1
  • Exceptions to universal screening at 36 0/7 - 37 6/7 weeks:1
    • Previous GBS infected newborn.1
    • GBS bacteriuria any of colony count at any time during current preganncy.1
    • Positive GBS culture performed earlier for preterm labor or PPROM.1
  • Universal screening 36 0/7 - 37 6/7 weeks.1
  • Preterm labor.1
  • Preterm prelabor ROM.1
  • Previous GBS infected newborn.1
  • GBS bacteriuria any of colony count at any time during current preganncy.1
  • Positive GBS culture performed earlier for preterm labor or PPROM.1
  • Not a subsitute for universal GBS screening at 36 0/7 - 37 6/7 weeks:1
    • Not as accurate as does not have 18-24 hour enrichment broth incubation before NAAT analysis that is used in universal screening.1
    • Cannot provide antibiotic senstivities so not as useful with high-risk PEN allergy.1
  • Could use for women in labor with unknown GBS status and no other risk factors for antbiotic prophylaxis.1
  • 2 options:1
    • GBS culture within 5 weeks of planned delivery.1
    • Empiric GBS antibiotic prophylaxis.1

Treatment

  • Previous pregnancy history:1
    • Previous GBS infected newborn.1
  • Current pregnancy hsitory:1
    • Postive GBS culture unless prelabor C/S with intact membranes.1
    • GBS bacteriuria any of colony count at any time during current preganncy.1
  • Intrapartum:1
    • Unknown GBS status:1
      • Preterm labor with risk preterm birth < 37 0/7 weeks.1
      • Preterm prelabor ROM.1
      • ROM ≥ 18 hours.1
      • Intrapartum fever (100.4°F/38°C)
        • Needs poly-microbial broad spectrum anitbiotics with GBS coverage (eg, ampicllin and gentamicin)1
      • Intrapartum NAAT positive.1
      • Intrapartum NAAT negative but other of these risk factors.1
      • GBS colonization in previous pregnancy.1
        • 50% chance GBS colonization current pregnancy.1

        • Empiric GBS antibiotic prophylaxis is option.1

  • IV penicillin 1st line 1
    • IV ampicillin acceptable.1
  • IV cefazolin if low risk or uncertain risk penicillin allergy.1
  • IV clindamycin if high risk penicillin allergy and GBS susceptible to clindamycin.1
  • IV vancomycin only alternative if high risk penicillin allergy and GBS not susceptible to clindamycin.1
    • Use weight/renal fuction based dose 1
      • 20 mg/kg IV every 8 hours.1
      • Max 2 g per dose.1

    • Consider penicillin allergy testing if possible IgE mediated allergy or unknown severity of allergy.1

    Oral or IM antibiotics antepartum or intrapartum not acceptable.1

    Antibiotic duration ≥ 4 hours most effective but 2 hours shown to decrease vaginal colony counts and neonatal sepsis.1

  • GBS prophylaxis in labor:1
    • GBS bacteriuria of any colony count at any time during pregnancy.1
  • Treat GBS bacteriuria prenatally:1
    • Symptomatic at any colony count.1
    • Asymptomatic if colony count > 105 CFU/ml (asymptomatic bacteriuria).1
      • Decreases risk of pyelonephritis, low birth weight (< 2500 grams), preterm birth.1
  • Don't treat GBS bacteriuria prenatally:1
    • Asymptomatic if colony count < 105 CFU/ml.1

If allergic to penicillin lab needs to give clindamycin sensitivity for GBS prophylaxis in labor. Clindamycin is metabolized in liver and concentrated poorly in urine so use other antibiotics for bacteriuria. Clindamycin useful for bloodstream and soft tissue infections.1
