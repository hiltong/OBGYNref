Breastfeeding

  • Rates U.S.:
    • Increasing, greater than 83% aty birth yet only 25% exclusively breastfeeding at 6 months.4
  • Maternal benefits:
    • Decrease risk of breast cancer, ovarian cancer, diabetes mellitus, hypertensive heart disease.4
  • ACOG recommends:
    • Exclusive breastfeeding ffirst 6 months then continue breastfeeding and introduce other foods.4

Problems

  • Timing:
    • Between postpartum day 3-4, can be 24-49 hours later with c-section.4
  • Symptoms:
    • Can be more severe withprimiparity, large amounts of intravenous fluids during labor, history of premenstrual breast tenderness, history of breast surgery.4
    • Breast and nipple can cause difficulties with latching.4
    • Management:
    • Supportive if mild symptoms and np problems latching.4
    • If severe reverse pressure techniques to decrease edema ans improve latch.4
  • Milk supply probably adequate if:
    • Feeding 8–12 times per day or about every 2-3 hours. Human milk easily digested so frequent feedings not a sign of insufficient milk supply. 4
    • Begin gaining weight by 4-5 days and exceed birth weight by 10–14 days.4
    • 6–8 wet diapers per day.4
  • Signs milk supply possibly not adequate:
    • Jaundice, insufficient wet diapers, lethargy, inconsolability, unchanged stool color (not bright yellow by day 5), lack of steady weight gain by day 4.4
  • Galactagogues not be considered a first-line therapy.4
  • Sheehan syndrome
  • Symptoms:
    • Pink/shiny/flaky/painful nipple/areola 4
  • Treatment:
    • Nipple:
      • Topical antifungal (miconazole and clotrimazole can inhibit Staphylococcus growth).4
      • Difficult to treat: Oral fluconazole 200 mg then 100 mg daily x 7-10 days (avoid use with erythromycin).4
    • Infant:
      • Nystatin suspension or miconazole oral gel.4
  • Risk factors:
    • Milk stasis (oversupply, latch difficulties, skipped feedings).4
    • Nipple injury.4
  • Treatment:
    • Milk removal (breastfeeding, express).4
    • Antibiotics:
      • Dicloxacillin (penicillinase-resistant penicillin) 500 mg QID x 10-14 days.4
      • Penicillin allergic:
        • Cephalexin (Keflex) 500 mg QID x 10-14 days.4
        • Clindamycin 300 mg QID x 10-14 days.4
    • Poor response consider:
      • Breast abscess.4
      • MRSA (ask about personal or household MRSA infections)4
  • Possible causes include differential diagnosis latch issues, pump trauma, dermatoses, infection, vasospasm, allodynia/functional pain, oversupply, ducts, and infant ankyloglossia.4
  • Can be associated with postpartum depression.4

Medications

  • Drugs and Lactation Database (LactMed) [Internet].4
  • IV iodinated contrast: Breastfeeding should not be interrupted. “Pump and dump” no longer recommended (amount of contrast absorbed by the infant < 0.01 %).4
  • IV gadolinium contrast: Breastfeeding should not be interrupted. (< 0.04% excreted into the breast milk within the first 24 hours of which the infant will absorb less than 1%. 5
  • Advise not to breastfeed:
    • Illicit drugs (e.g. methamphetamines,cocaine, phencyclidine)4
  • Discourage use while breastfeeding:
    • Marijuana:4
      • Insufficient data to determine effects on breastfeeding.4
      • May interfere with care of child.4
    • Tobacco smoking:4
      • Secondhand smoke harmful (infant respiratory allergies and increased risk of sudden infant death).4
  • Limited and occasional use:
    • Alcohol:4
      • Risks:
        • Milk concentration is similar to the concentration in plasma.4
        • Can impair milk ejection reflex and milk production.4
        • Can impair infant motor development.4
      • Occasional use:
        • ≤ 8 ounces of wine or ≤ 2 ounces of liquor or ≤ 2 beers.4,6
        • Wait at least 2 hours after the alcohol intake.4
  • Encouraged to breastfeed:
    • Methadone or buprenorphine.1,4
      • Only small amount in breast milk.4
      • May decrease neonatal abstinence syndrome.4

Etc.

  • Breastfeeding contraindicated:
    • Infant
      • Galactosemia (can't digest galactose in milk)1
    • Maternal infections
      • Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection1
      • Human T-cell lymphotropic virus type I or type II1
      • Active untreated tuberculosis1
      • Varicella1
      • Active herpes simplex virus lesions on the nipple1
    • Maternal medications
      • Lead levels ≥ 40 mcg/dl or lead level > 20 mcg/dL and infant lead level ≥ 5 mcg/dL2
      • Lithium3
      • Methotrexate?
      • Radioactive iodine within 120 days?
      • Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors)?
    • Maternal substance abuse
      • Alcohol abusee4
      • Street Drugs (e.g. Methamphetamines,cocaine,phencyclidine)4
  • Breastfeeding not contraindicated:
    • Maternal infections
      • Hepatitis C & B?
    • Maternal medications
      • Coumadin?
      • IV iodinated contrast4
      • IV gadolinium contrast5
      • Methadone or buprenorphine1
