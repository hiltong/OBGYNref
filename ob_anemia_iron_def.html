Iron Deficiency Anemia in Pregnancy

Background

  • 1st trimester:
    • Hgb < 11 g/dl or Hct < 33%.1
  • 2nd trimester:
    • Hgb < 10.5 g/dl or Hct < 32%.1
  • 3rd trimester:
    • Hgb < 11 g/dl or Hct < 33%.1
  • Presumptive.1
  • Serum ferritin < 30 mcg/L:
    • Highest sensitivity & specificity. 1
  • Iron deficiency anemia:
    • Iron level: Decreased.1
    • TBIC: Increased.1
    • Iron level: Decreased.1
    • Transferrin saturation (Serum iron/TBIC ratio): < 18%.1
  • Thalassemia:
    • Iron level: Normal.1
    • TBIC: Normal.1
    • Iron level: Normal.1
    • Transferrin saturation (Serum iron/TBIC ratio): Normal.1
  • Anemia of chronic disease:
    • Iron level: Decreased.1
    • TBIC: Decreased.1
    • Iron level: Increased.1
    • Transferrin saturation (Serum iron/TBIC ratio): > 18%.1

Management

  • Prenatal vitamin containing iron to meet needs of pregnancy.1
  • Iron supplementation in addition to prenatal vitamins.1
    • Oral iron:
      • Consider once daily and every other day as may increase absorption and decrease side effects.2
      • Sustained release (e.g. SlowFe) or enteric coated may be poorly absorbed.1
    • Intravenous iron after 1st trimester & postpartum:
      • If don't tolerate oral iron as no GI side effects.1
      • If poor response to oral iron as more effective.1
      • If severe anemia later in pregnancy as more rapid correction.1
      • If anatomic conditions such as some bariatric surgeries (e.g. Roux-en-Y bypass) or some medical conditions such as inflammatory bowel disease that interfere with oral iron absorption.1
  • Iron sucrose (Venofer):
    • Most history of use in pregnancy.2
    • Requires multiple doses (e.g. 200 mg x 5 over 14 days).4
  • Ferric carboxymaltose (Injectafer):
    • 1-2 doses (750 mg q week x 1-2).3
    • Can cause hypophosphatemia but clinically significant hypophosphatemia not reported in pregnancy.2

All seem equally effective and have a similar safety profile.2

  • Hgb < 6 g/dl.1
