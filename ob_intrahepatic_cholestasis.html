Intrahepatic Cholestasis of Pregnancy

  • Probably genetic susceptibly interacting with environmental and hormonal influences.2
  • Previous intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy.2
    • Maybe as high as 90%.2
  • Hepatobiliary disease (hepatitis, nonalcoholic liver cirrhosis, gallstones and cholecystitis and nonalcoholic pancreatitis).
  • Advanced maternal age.2
  • Multiple gestations.2
  • Familial.2
  • Pruritus;
    • New-onset in second half of pregnancy.2
    • Can be generalized but predominately palms and soles.2
    • Worse at night.2
    • No rash but can have excoriations from scratching.2
  • Elevated total serum bile acids:
    • >10 μmol/L.2
  • Labs to order if suspected:
    • Serum total bile acid (random).2
    • Liver transaminase levels (sometimes elevated but not needed for diagnosis).2
    • Hepatitis C if risk factors.2
  • Repeat labs:
    • Don’t need weekly total bile acid testing but can increase rapidly near term so in severe disease may help plan delivery.2
    • Pruritus can occur several weeks before increase serum bile acid so if labs are normal and suspicious should repeat labs.2
  • Other causes of increased bile acids:
    • Drug-induced cholestasis (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, phenothiazines, ampicillin), Primary sclerosing cholangitis (associated with inflammatory bowel disease), Bacterial, fungal, and viral infections (eg, Ebstein-Barr virus and cytomegalovirus), Primary biliary cholangitis, Obstructive bile duct lesion, Liver tumor, Hepatic amyloidosis, Lymphoma and solid organ malignancies, Hepatic sarcoidosis, Autoimmune hepatitis, Idiopathic adulthood ductopenia.2
  • Maternal:
    • Possibly preeclampsia.2
      • Especially with total bile acid levels of ≥40 μmol/L. Usually occurred 2-4 weeks after intrahepatic cholestasis diagnosesed and proteinuria was first finding.2
    • Possibly risk for later hepatobiliary diseases (e.g. chronic hepatitis, hepatitis C, cirrhosis , and cholangitis).2
  • Fetal:
    • Preterm delivery (spontaneous and iatrogenic).2
      • Bile acids seem to activate myometrial oxytocin receptors.2
    • Meconium-stained amniotic fluid.2
    • Stillbirth.2
      • Maybe secondary to bile acids causing a fetal arrhythmia or vasospasm of the placental chorionic surface vessels.2
      • Risks seem related to total bile acid level:2
        • >10 μmol/L is usually diagnosis level.2
        • ≥40 μmol/L has sometimes been a cutoff for risk but may be some risk at lower levels.2
        • ≥100 μmol/L highest risk level.2
  • Ursodeoxycholic acid (Ursodiol) first-line:2
    • Improves pruritus in 1-2 weeks.2
    • Improves lab abnormalities in 3-4 weeks2
    • Up to 25% have mild nausea and dizziness.2
    • Uncertain if improves perinatal outcomes2
    • No known fetal effects.2
    • Dosing:
      • Starting dose 10–15 mg/kg per day divided into 2 or 3 daily doses.
        • Commonly 300 mg BID or TID or 500 mg BID.2
      • Titrate to max dose of 21 mg/kg per day if no improvement.2
  • Alternatives:
    • S-adenosyl-methionine.2
    • Cholestyramine.2
    • Rifampin.2
  • Start when delivery would be done if abnormal.2
    • Questioned if antepartum fetal testing useful because the cause of stillbirth possibly a sudden event rather than placental insufficiency. When occurs stillbirth usually not associated with fetal growth restriction, oligohydramnios, or abnormal placental histology (other than meconium staining).2
  • Need continuous fetal monitoring during labor.2
  • “Risk-stratification based on patient-specific factors, including the total bile acid levels, in a shared decision-making model.”2
    • Offer 36 0/7 - 39 0/7 weeks if total bile acid levels of <100 μmol/ 1,2
      • Total bile acid levels of <40 μmol/L consider later range.2
      • Total bile acid levels of ≥40 μmol/L consider earlier range.2
    • Offer 36 0/7 weeks if total bile acid levels of ≥100 μmol/L1,2
    • Consider 34 - 36 week if total bile acid levels ≥100 μmol/L and:2
      • Severe maternal pruritus not relieved with medication.2
      • Previous stillbirth < 36 weeks due to intrahepatic cholestasis.2
      • Worsening hepatic function with preexisting or acute hepatic disease.2
    • Antenatal corticosteroids if delivery before 37 0/7 weeks (if not previously given)2
  • Suspected intrahepatic cholestasis 37 to 38 weeks and bile acid results not available:2
    • Decision to deliver should be based on shared decision making with patient. Decision easier if elevated transaminase or intrahepatic cholestasis in a previous pregnancy. Don’t deliver <37 weeks of gestation with suspected intrahepatic cholestasis without elevated bile acids.2
  • Repeat liver function tests if symptoms don't resolve.2
