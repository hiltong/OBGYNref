Gestaional Hypertension & Preeclampsia

  • Gestaional hypertension without severe-range blood pressures:1
    • Systolic BP ≥ 140 mm Hg or diastolic BP ≥ 90 mm Hg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart after 20 weeks of gestation in a woman with a previously normal blood pressure1
    • No proteinuria (if proteinuira use preeclampsia).1
    • No severe features (instead of gestational hypertension with severe features use preeclampsia with severe features).1

      • 50% develop proteinuria or severe features.1

  • Gestaional hypertension with severe-range blood pressures:1
    • Systolic BP ≥ 160 or diastolic BP ≥ 110 on 2 occasions at least 4 hours apart unless BP treated before 4 hours.1
    • No proteinuria (if proteinuira use preeclampsia).1
    • No other severe features (instead of gestational hypertension with other severe features use preeclampsia with severe features).1
  • Preeclampsia without severe features:1
    • Systolic BP ≥ 140 mm Hg or diastolic BP ≥ 90 mm Hg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart after 20 weeks of gestation in a woman with a previously normal blood pressure1
    • Proteinuria:1
      • ≥ 300 mg on 24 hour urine 1
      • Protein/creatinine ratio ≥ 0.31
      • Dipstick 2+ (use only if other quantitative methods not available)1
  • Preeclampsia with severe features:1
    • Systolic BP ≥ 160 or diastolic BP ≥ 110 on 2 occasions at least 4 hours apart unless BP treated before 4 hours.1
    • Platelet count < 100,000/microL.1
    • Elevated liver enzymes (AST & ALT) ≥ twice upper limit of normal or severe persistent right upper quadrant or epigastric pain unresponsive to medication and not accounted for by alternative diagnoses or both.1
    • Serum creatinine concentration > 1.1 mg/dL or a doubling of the serum creatinine in absence of other renal disease.1
    • Pulmonary edema.1
    • New-onset headache unresponsive to medication (acetaminophen) and not accounted for by other diagnoses.1
    • Visual disturbances.1

    Older criteria included proteinuria > 5 g/24 hours and fetal growth restriction.1

  • HELP (hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, low platelets)1
    • LDH ≥ 600 IU/L1
    • Platelet count < .,000/microL.1
    • Elevated liver enzymes (AST & ALT) ≥ twice upper limit of normal or severe persistent right upper quadrant or epigastric pain unresponsive to medication and not accounted for by alternative diagnoses or both.1

      • 30% occurs postpartum.1

      15% lack hypertension or proteinuira. Presenting symptoms 90% cases are RUQ pain and malaise and 50% cases vomiting.1

  • Eclampsia:1
    • 78–83% of cases preceded by premonitory signs of cerebral irritation.1
    • 20–38% don not have classic signs of preeclmpsia.1
    • May occur if untreated in preeclampsia (1.9%) or severe preeclampsia (3.2%).1
  • Amaurosis fugax (temporary blindness):1
    • May occur with preeclampsia or eclampsia.1
    • May last several hours to a week.1
  • PRES (Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome):1
    • May occur with preeclampsia or eclampsia.1
    • Headache, seizure, altered consciousness or confusion, or vision loss or deficit.1
    • Diagnosis by MRI with vasogenic edema and hyperintensities in the posterior aspects of the brain.1
  • RCVS (Reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome):1
    • May be confused with preeclampsia or eclampsia.1
    • Reversible multifocal narrowing of the arteries of the brain with characterized by a thunderclap headache and sometimes focal neurologic deficits or seizure.1
  • With preeclampsia aspartate aminotransferase (AST) higher than alanine aminotransferase (ALT) secondary to periportal necrosis.1
  • With parenchymal liver disease ALT usually is higher than AST.1
  • LDH maybe elevated secondary to hepatic damage or hemolysis.1
  • < 34 0/7 weeks 1
    • Maternal:1
      • Uncontrolled severe-range BP not responsive to medication.1
      • Persistent headaches unresponsive to treatment.1
      • Visual disturbances, motor deficit or altered sensorium.1
      • Epigastric pain or right upper pain unresponsive to analgesics.1
      • Stroke.1
      • Myocardial infarction.1
      • Renal dysfunction (serum creatinine greater than 1.1 mg/dL or twice baseline).1
      • Pulmonary edema.1
      • Suspected acute placental abruption or vaginal bleeding in the absence of placenta previa.1
      • HELLP syndrome.1
      • Eclampsia.1
    • Fetus:1
      • Fetal death or fetus without expectation for survival.1
      • Abnormal fetal testing.1
      • Persistent reversed end-diastolic flow in the umbilical artery.1
  • 34 0/7 weeks:1
    • Preeclampsia with severe features with stable maternal and fetal condition1
    • Expectant management of stable preeclampsia with severe features can prolong pregnancy by 1–2 weeks.1
  • 37 0/7 weeks:1
    • Gestational hypertension or preeclampsia without severe features.1
  • Therapeutic: 5-9 mg/dl.1
  • Loss deep tendon reflexes: > 9 mg/dl.1
  • Respiratory depression: > 12 mg/dL.1
  • Cardiac arrest: > 30 mg/dl.1
  • Calcium gluconate 10% solution, 10 ml intravenous over 3 minutes + intravenous furosemide to increase urinary excretion.1
  • Sodium amobarbital (250 mg IV in 3 minutes).1
  • Thiopental.1
  • Phenytoin (1,250 mg IV at a rate of 50 mg/minute).1
  • Endotracheal intubation with assisted ventilation.1
  • Head imaging.1
  • Without severe features: at diagnois, 2x/weekly.3
  • With severe features: at diagnois, daily.3
